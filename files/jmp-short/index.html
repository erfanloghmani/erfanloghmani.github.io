Methodology overview — Value of Experimental and Observational Data

Ranking headlines from embeddings

  • Preference unit: headline pairs within the same test–image (Ye, Yoganarasimhan, and Zheng, 2024Ye, Z., Yoganarasimhan, H., and Zheng, Y. (2024). LOLA: LLM-Assisted Online Learning Algorithm for Content Experiments. arXiv preprint arXiv:2406.02611.)
  • Represent each candidate by embedding the concatenated abstract + headline
  • Three pipelines on top of those embeddings
    • Logistic regression on experimental pairs (ranking objective)
    • Ridge CTR prediction on all experimental arms
    • Same CTR prediction on observational data (one arm per test–image)
  • Split at the test–image level; drop eval pairs with any training-headline overlap
  • Evaluate by ROC-AUC on held-out pairs with distinguishable CTRs (p < 0.05)
  • Goal: given a news abstract, can the model rank candidate headlines by CTR?
Erfan Loghmani 12 / 24
We focus on headlines with the image held fixed, as in Ye et al. Comparable
pairs are formed within a test–image. Embeddings of abstract plus headline feed
three pipelines: logistic ranking on experimental pairs, CTR prediction on full
experimental data, and the same CTR model on an observational subsample. Splits
are at the test–image level with zero headline leakage into evaluation. We score
models by ROC-AUC on held-out pairs that are statistically distinguishable.

Once we have both sources, can we beat using either alone? Pooling two CTR

Combining both sources — Common domain

Combine at the prediction level to bring both models to the same domain $\Pr(a \succ b)$

  • Exploit bias–variance complementarity
    • Observational CTR: biased, but low variance (lots of data)
    • Experimental logistic: unbiased, but high variance when $x$ is small
    • Weight toward observational when experiments are scarce; shift to experimental as $x$ grows
  • Native outputs differ → map both to the same domain $\Pr(a \succ b)$
    • Logistic: delivers this probability (and a variance) directly → $\hat\theta_U$
    • CTR model: point CTR + SE per headline; assume ≈ Normal, Monte-Carlo the two arms → implied $\Pr(a \succ b)$ and variance → $\hat\theta_B$
Erfan Loghmani 16 / 24
We combine predictions, not parameters. Observational CTR is biased but precise;
logistic is unbiased but noisy when experiments are scarce. To mix them we map
both to Pr(a beats b): logistic already lives there; CTR is converted by Monte
Carlo sampling of the two arms. That gives theta-hat U and theta-hat B.

Combining both sources — Inverse-variance weights

Bias-corrected IVW chooses how much to trust each estimate

  • Convex combination of the common-domain estimates
    • $$\hat\theta_{\mathrm{new}} = w\,\hat\theta_B + (1-w)\,\hat\theta_U$$
  • Choose $w$ to minimize MSE; $\hat\theta_U$ unbiased and the two branches uncorrelated
    • $$\mathrm{MSE} = w^2\mathrm{Bias}(\hat\theta_B)^2 + w^2\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B) + (1-w)^2\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_U)$$
  • Optimal weight
    • $$w^\star = \frac{\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_U)}{\mathrm{Bias}(\hat\theta_B)^2 + \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_B) + \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_U)}$$
  • Behavior matches the tradeoff
    • Few experiments → large $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta_U)$ → $w^\star \to 1$ (lean observational)
    • Large observational bias → $w^\star \to 0$ (lean experimental)
    • As experimental sample grows, weight slides from $B$ toward $U$
Erfan Loghmani 17 / 24
Once both branches estimate the same win probability, form a convex combination
and pick the weight that minimizes MSE. The closed form is a bias-corrected
inverse-variance weight: noisy experiments borrow strength from observational
data; large observational bias defers to the experiment.

Conclusion

How a firm should use its data to optimize unstructured marketing content

  • Three takeaways
    • Observational alignment alone can be problematic because fine-tuned model might internalize confounders
    • Experimental data help mainly when the model uses a comparative objective, rather than a performance prediction objective
    • With both sources, combine them: bias-corrected IVW beats either alone
  • Strategic testing matters when budgets are tight
    • Optimal allocation depends on the time horizon
    • Over longer windows, prioritize tests where historical and experimental predictions disagree
  • Limitations / future work
    • Evaluate on other data sources and potentially other modalities
    • IVW is robust without structural assumptions; task-specific fusion might use problem structure to improve

This paper asked how a firm should use its data to optimize unstructured marketing
content generation by AI. Our answer has three parts. First, optimizing a generative
model on historical outcomes alone can backfire, teaching it to reproduce confounders
rather than quality. Second, experimental data are valuable, but mostly when paired
with a model that targets the ranking objective, not performance prediction; the
modeling choice matters more than the experimental variation itself. Third, when a
firm holds both experimental and observational data, the two are best combined rather
than used in isolation. Our bias-corrected, inverse-variance estimator does exactly
this, leaning on abundant observational data when experiments are scarce and on the
experiment as it accumulates, and it outperforms either source alone, surpassing full
observational performance with experiments on just over 1% of decisions. Finally,
when experimental budgets are strictly limited, strategic test selection becomes
critical. We find that the optimal allocation of this budget depends heavily on the
time horizon; specifically, over longer collection windows, prioritizing tests where
historical and experimental predictions most disagree emerges as the most effective
strategy.

These results carry a practical lesson for firms weighing whether and how to
experiment. Firms often have an abundant set of historical observational data, while
experimentation is costly. In our time-based simulation, no amount of pure
experimentation beats a model trained on historical data once the opportunity cost of
testing is counted. Only by combining the two sources does a small amount of
experimentation yield a significant net gain.

Our study has limitations that point to directions for future work. Validating methods
that combine experimental and observational data requires settings where the full
experimental variation is observed, since that is what reveals whether the correlations
in observational data are genuine or spurious. Such data is scarce, and we were able
to test our approach only on the Upworthy archive, which varies textual headlines over
news articles. More data sources are needed to assess how the approach generalizes,
particularly to settings with richer unstructured variation such as images, video, or
multimodal creative elements. Additionally, while our inverse-variance weighting
provides a robust and tractable combination mechanism without structural assumptions,
future research could explore how task-specific structure might yield fusion methods
that more effectively mitigate confounding and combine data sources.
